    Section 4.3 The Logistic Model

    In the last section we discussed linear flow rates, and the specific cases which result in exponential behavior of a stock over time. It is possible for flow rates to be non-linear, which in general result in more complicated stock behavior. The purpose of this activity is to introduce a common non-linear flow rate, and tools for analyzing the behavior of these models.
    The objectives of this activity are that you can...

    Objectives

    1. describe the similarities and differences of the exponential and logistic models.
    2. analyze the types of feedback present in a logistic model.
    3. sketch a graph of a stock’s value over time for a stock that follows a logistic model.

    Let’s get to work....

    In the previous section we introduced exponential models, which arise when the net flow rate for a stock is linear (and not constant). There is a certain level of simplicity to these models because
    1. There is always a single equilibrium value for the stock (when the linear net flow rate is equal to \(0\)), and
    2. The overall feedback is always either amplifying or stabilizing (depending on whether the linear net flow rate increases or decreases as the the stock value increases).
    These observations are illustrated in Figure 4.3.1.
    If we consider flow rates that are not linear, these observations are no longer true. In general, there could be multiple equilibrium values of the stock, and the overall feedback affect may depend on the stock’s value. While this makes the model more complicated, these complexities may be desired in order to obtain a more accurate model.
    Let’s consider modeling a population that is increasing over time. A good first model would be to use an amplifying exponential model, such as \(\text{Net Flow} = 2P\text{.}\) This does a nice job at capturing the tendency for reproduction (Net Flow) to increase with an increase in Population (P). Depending on the purpose of your model, however, there may be one large flaw. With a positive initial value, this is an amplifying exponential model which is increasing, and the line graph for the population over time would look something like Figure 4.3.2.
    While the pattern of increase may be accurate at first, this graph shows that the population will continue to increase more and more rapidly without end. It does not account for factors such as limited resources, which at some point should start to have a stabilizing affect on the population. The logistic model takes this into account.

    Definition 4.3.3.

    A stock-flow model follows a logistic model if the net flow rate of the stock \(S\) has the form
    \begin{equation*} \text{Net Flow } = rS\left(1-\frac{S}{C}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The constant \(r\) represents the growth rate of the stock when the stock value is very small. The constant \(C\) represents the carrying capacity (the maximum population that the environment can sustain).
    Note that while the logistic model has an amplifying factor (\(rS\)), it also has a stabilizing factor \(\left(1-\frac{S}{C}\right)\text{.}\) That is, as \(S\) increases, \(rS\) also increases, but \(\left(1-\frac{S}{C}\right)\) decreases. We’ll analyze the overall affect of these competing factors now.
    Suppose we have a stock-flow model of a population \(P\) with a single inflow of \(0.5P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\text{,}\) and no outflow. That is, the population’s net flow rate is
    \begin{equation*} \text{Net Flow } =0.5P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The growth rate when the population is very small is \(0.5\text{,}\) and the carrying capacity of this population is \(100\) individuals. The net flow rate is non-linear, and the graph of the net flow rate with respect to the population (stock) value is given in Figure 4.3.4.
