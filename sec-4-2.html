    Definition 4.2.3.

    An exponential relationship is a relationship whose relative change during any single unit of time is constant. If the constant relative change is greater than one, we call it exponential growth. If the the relative change is between zero and one, we call it exponential decay.
    As we saw in the table calculuations above, exponential relationships can be good for describing models that either
    • Increase or decrease very rapidly, or
    • Increase or decrease very gradually.
    A transformation of a line graph is a new line graph obtained from stretching, shifting, or reflecting the original line graph. Since transformations of exponential relationships still exhibit the general properties described above, we will also refer to transformations of exponentials relationships as exhibiting exponential behavior. It will be useful to be able to visualize the line graphs representing this behavior.

    Back to work....

    Now we will look at how exponential relationships arise in stock-flow models. Suppose we have a stock \(S\) with a single inflow of \(2S\) units per second. If we assume an initial stock value of \(100\) units, then after the first second we would have an inflow of \(2\cdot 100 =200\) units, meaning our total stock after the first second would be \(300\) units. If we continue to take measurements after each second

    Note that we are treating this as a discrete system, in which measurements are taken at equally spaced time intervals. This is opposed to a continuous system, in which measurements are taken continuously over a time interval.
    , we would have the measurements recorded in Table 4.2.4 after the first four seconds.
    This table should remind us of Table 4.2.1 that we analyzed earlier: the stock’s value is increasing rapidly, but if we compute the relative change between each time interval we get the constant \(3\text{.}\) The inflow to this stock increases in an exponential way! This behavior is the result of the constant multiplication of the stock’s value \(S\) by the factor \(2\text{.}\) Any flow rate of the form \(cS\text{,}\) where \(c\) is a non-zero constant, will result in exponential behavior of the stock’s value. If the flow rate has the form \(cS+b\text{,}\) where \(b\) is a non-zero constant, this will result in a transformation of exponential behavior. Because of this, we give flow rates of this form a special name.

    Definition 4.2.5.

    If a stock \(S\) has a flow rate of the form \(cS+b\text{,}\) where \(c\) and \(b\) are constants, we say that flow rate is linear.
    As we’ve just seen, linear flow rates in which the constant \(c\) is non-zero result in exponential behavior of a stock’s value. We saw in the previous section that it is the net flow rate of a stock that determines a stock’s behavior over time, so the following fact is relevant.
    If a stock’s inflow and outflow rates are linear, then its net flow rate is also linear.
    Let’s return to our example of the stock \(S\) with inflow rate \(2S\text{,}\) and suppose also there is a single outflow of \(3S-6\) units per second. Then the net flow rate for the stock is
    \begin{equation*} 2S-(3S-6) = -S+6 \text{ units per second}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It is important to note that the negative sign in front of \(S\) does not mean that the net flow is always negative! It depends on \(S\text{:}\)
    • If \(S=5\text{,}\) the net flow will be positive.
    • If \(S=7\text{,}\) the net flow will be negative.
    • If \(S=6\text{,}\) the net flow will be \(0\text{.}\) That is, the stock is in equilibrium.
